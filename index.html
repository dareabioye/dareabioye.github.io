Conducted an end-to-end predictive analytics study on a Paris residential housing dataset, building machine learning models to identify key price drivers and forecast property values. The pipeline included rigorous data cleaning (IQR outlier capping, median imputation, duplicate removal), feature engineering of 12+ new variables (price per sqft, property age, renovation status, bedroom density), log transformation to address skewness, and StandardScaler normalisation. Two regression models — Linear Regression and Random Forest — were evaluated and compared using R² and MSE on an 80/20 train-test split, with Recursive Feature Elimination with Cross-Validation (RFECV) for hyperparameter tuning.

// Key Findings & Business Insights

  • Random Forest (R²=0.66, MSE=0.33) outperformed Linear Regression (R²=0.61, MSE=0.38) — recommended as the preferred model for capturing non-linear property price relationships
  • Enhanced feature engineering lifted R² from 0.56 to 0.91 — a 36-percentage point improvement, demonstrating the power of domain-informed feature creation
  • Living area (sqft) and construction grade are the strongest price predictors (correlations ~0.65–0.70); bedrooms and floors show weaker direct relationships
  • Renovated properties command ~12% price premium over non-renovated equivalents — key investment signal for real estate strategy
  • Inverse relationship between floors and total sqft reflects Paris's urban zoning constraints (PLU regulations), where multi-floor buildings are constrained in footprint
  • Bedroom-price relationship plateaus beyond 4–5 bedrooms, suggesting diminishing returns — bathrooms add more value per unit than bedrooms in the Paris market

Designed and trained six feedforward neural networks to classify handwritten digits (0–9) from the MNIST dataset, conducting a rigorous comparative study across activation functions (ReLU vs Tanh), optimisers (Adam vs SGD), and regularisation techniques (L2). Each model shared the same architecture — 784-unit input, three hidden layers (512→256→128 neurons), and a 10-class Softmax output — to isolate the effect of each hyperparameter. Models were trained with early stopping (patience=3), batch size 64, and up to 50 epochs. Evaluation included training/validation curves, confusion matrices, and sample prediction visualisations.

// Key Findings & Technical Insights

  • ReLU + Adam achieved the highest accuracy (97.92%) and fastest training time (18.89s) — aligned with Glorot & Bengio (2010) on ReLU's non-saturating gradient propagation advantage
  • Tanh consistently underperformed ReLU across all configurations, with the largest gap under L2 regularisation — confirming Tanh's susceptibility to vanishing gradients in deeper networks
  • SGD reduced overfitting gap between training and validation accuracy compared to Adam, despite significantly slower convergence (126s vs 20s for Tanh)
  • L2 regularisation (λ=0.001) improved generalisation and controlled overfitting, particularly when combined with early stopping
  • Ethical analysis flagged MNIST's clean, balanced nature as a limitation for real-world deployment — noting Buolamwini & Gebru (2018) findings on AI classification bias in production environments

Designed and implemented a Monte Carlo simulation of 40,000 randomly generated consumer records across six age groups (20–70), comparing two probabilistic models of purchasing behaviour. The weighted model assigned age-dependent purchase probabilities (15%→65%), reflecting the real-world tendency of older consumers to purchase more frequently. The uniform model applied an equal 40% probability across all groups as a baseline control. Conditional probability P(Purchase | Age=70) was calculated analytically using Bayes' theorem and verified computationally, with full mathematical workings and comparative visualisation.

// Key Mathematical Findings & Business Insights

  • Weighted model: P(Purchase | Age=70) = 0.6494, derived as P(Purchase ∩ Age=70) / P(Age=70) = 0.1071 / 0.1650 — consistent with the 65% prior probability assigned to 70-year-olds
  • Unweighted model: P(Purchase | Age=70) = 0.3989 — confirming that removing age-based weighting collapses all conditional probabilities toward the global mean of ~0.40
  • Weighted model produces a 63% uplift in purchase probability for the 70+ age group vs uniform assumption — critical signal for age-targeted marketing strategy
  • Both models produce identical overall purchase rates (~0.40) but diverge sharply at the subgroup level — demonstrating Simpson's Paradox risk when using only aggregate statistics
  • Scatter plot analysis confirmed linear monotonic increase in weighted purchase probability (0.14 at 20 → 0.65 at 70) versus flat ~0.40 line for unweighted model across all groups
